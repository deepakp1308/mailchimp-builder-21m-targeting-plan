Strengths to Preserve

Before fixing what's broken, the plan does six things right that should be carried into v2:

  • Clustering as discovery, not prioritization. The plan explicitly warns against using cluster membership as the targeting answer. Final ranking comes from expected value, not cluster ID. This is correct and uncommon.
  • Time-based train/test splits. The plan rejects random splits as the main validation approach. Train on months 1-6, score at end of month 6, measure outcomes in months 7-9. This prevents leakage from future-to-past correlation.
  • Uplift modeling alongside propensity. The plan correctly distinguishes "who is likely to convert" (propensity) from "who would not have converted without intervention" (uplift). High-propensity customers may convert anyway.
  • Estimate quality labels. Every estimate must be labeled as Validated, Directionally Supported, Assumption Based, or Insufficient Data. This is exactly the kind of intellectual honesty that survives finance scrutiny.
  • Privacy guardrails. Hashed customer IDs only. No PII in outputs. This protects the work from compliance issues later.
  • Reason codes for every recommendation. Every targeting decision must come with plain-English explanation. This is what makes the work usable by GTM, CSM, and sales teams.
Bottom Line: The plan's analytical philosophy is right. The execution details have gaps that need to be filled before the $21M number can be defended. The improvements in this site are additive — they don't replace the plan's structure, they fill in the missing math, tooling, and validation that turn a smart analytical approach into a trustworthy business case.
