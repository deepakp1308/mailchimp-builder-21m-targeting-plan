Evaluation: Strengths, Critical Gaps, and Risks

An honest read on what the Builder $21M Targeting Agent plan gets right, where it has holes, and which gaps put the $21M projection most at risk.

Strengths to Preserve (6 items)

The original plan's analytical philosophy is correct. These elements should be carried into v2 unchanged:

1. Clustering as discovery, not prioritization

The plan explicitly warns against "Cluster 4 has high engagement and high revenue, so target it." Final ranking comes from expected value calculation, not cluster membership. This is correct and uncommon — most analytics projects make this mistake.

2. Time-based train/test splits

The plan rejects random splits as the primary validation approach: "Train on months 1-6, score customers at end of month 6, measure outcomes in months 7-9." This prevents leakage from future correlations contaminating training features.

3. Uplift modeling separate from propensity

The plan correctly distinguishes "probability of conversion" (propensity) from "incremental probability if we intervene" (uplift). High-propensity customers may convert anyway; the uplift question is which customers would not have converted without intervention. This is the right framing.

4. Estimate quality labels

Every estimate must be labeled as Validated, Directionally Supported, Assumption Based, or Insufficient Data. This kind of intellectual honesty survives finance scrutiny in a way that "the model says $21M" never does.

5. Privacy guardrails

Hashed customer IDs only. No PII in outputs. No emails, names, phone numbers, addresses. This protects the work from compliance and Trust & Safety issues that would otherwise surface during productionization.

6. Reason codes for every recommendation

Every targeting decision comes with a plain-English explanation ("starts campaigns but fails to send; high MRR; recent Builder friction"). This is what makes the work usable by GTM, CSM, and sales teams, not just data scientists.

Top 5 Highest-Impact Fixes (Where to Start)

If you only have time for five improvements before reporting back, do these in order:

Fix 1: Replace 12 × MRR with survival-based CLV

Use lifelines.KaplanMeierFitter to compute survival curves by segment (paid tier, tenure, ecommerce flag, MRR band). For each segment, integrate under the survival curve to get expected retention months. Multiply by MRR to get expected retention value. For paid customers with 50% retention at month 12, expected value is roughly 8 × MRR, not 12 × MRR.

Impact: Could swing the $21M projection by $5-8M depending on actual retention curves. This is the single highest-leverage fix.

Fix 2: Add MLflow + Great Expectations

MLflow tracks every model run with its hyperparameters, training data, metrics, and artifacts. Great Expectations defines data contracts (e.g., "current_mrr must be non-null, ≥0, and within 3 sigma of last week's distribution") and fails the pipeline if violated. Without these, the work is not reproducible or trustworthy.

Impact: Defensibility. Data science review will reject any model output that can't be reproduced or whose input data is unvalidated.

Fix 3: Define confidence discount math explicitly

Replace the undefined multiplier with: confidence_discount = (sample_size_score × treatment_evidence_score × calibration_score)^(1/3) where each component is in [0,1]. See the Improvements page for the full formula.

Impact: Credibility. Without a written formula, the confidence discount becomes "whatever produces a defensible-looking number" — the opposite of rigor.

Fix 4: Add Monte Carlo to the $21M waterfall

For each customer's EV, sample from a distribution over (P(success), uplift, revenue) rather than using point estimates. Run 10,000 iterations. Report P10/P50/P90 of the aggregated $21M projection. This produces a confidence band: "We project $14M-$24M with 80% confidence, median $19M" rather than "We project $21M."

Impact: Risk framing. Executives can make better decisions when they know the realistic range, not just the central estimate.

Fix 5: Handle customer overlap in sequencing

Add an assignment constraint to Phase 11: each customer is assigned to at most one initiative across all waves. The optimization is maximize sum(EV_ci × x_ci) subject to sum_i(x_ci) ≤ 1 for all customers c. Without this, the same customer's revenue is counted multiple times across waves.

Impact: Could overstate $21M by 15-25%. This is a "math error" that becomes obvious in scrutiny.

Hidden Risks (Things Not in the Plan But Should Be)

  • Survivorship bias in historical CLV. If retention curves are computed only on customers who are still in the dataset, you're systematically excluding the customers who churned. Use cohort-based survival from the original cohort, not the surviving population.
  • SUTVA violation in uplift modeling. The Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption says one customer's treatment doesn't affect another's outcome. In Mailchimp, if Builder improvements ship to all users (not just treatment group), historical "treatment vs. control" comparisons may not be valid. Watch for cross-contamination.
  • Goodhart's Law on activation metrics. If "campaign sent in 30 days" becomes the optimization target, the model may target customers whose first send was inevitable (high propensity, low uplift). This is why uplift modeling matters.
  • Concept drift in 2026. Klaviyo shipped Universal Content Spring 2026. Mailchimp's Builder behavior may shift as customers compare. Historical features may not predict future behavior the same way. Plan to retrain frequently.
  • The "easy first" trap. Wave 1 may be customers with high propensity AND high uplift, but those may also be the smallest cohort. Wave 4 ("strategic learning value") may actually be where $21M is hiding. Don't anchor on Wave 1 size as the indicator of success.
Net assessment: The plan's analytical philosophy is sound. The execution details have 15 critical gaps that need to be filled before the $21M number can be defended. The top 5 fixes address the most material issues: survival-based CLV (Fix 1), MLOps foundation (Fix 2), defined confidence math (Fix 3), Monte Carlo uncertainty (Fix 4), and overlap handling (Fix 5). Together they change the deliverable from "ambitious analytical project" to "defensible $21M business case."
